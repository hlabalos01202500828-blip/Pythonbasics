🌍 Origin & Overview of Music

⭐ Learning Objectives
Learn various musical styles and genres from diverse cultures and history. Explore the four orchestra families. Examine how instrument combinations and styles affect mood, purpose, and emotional impact.

Archaeological Evidence

40,000 Years Ago

Archaeological evidence suggests humans were already creating and using musical instruments at least 40,000 years ago. Early instruments made from organic materials (wood and bone) decomposed over time, leaving limited physical evidence.

The Bear Femur Flute (1995)

A perforated bear femur found in Slovenia in 1995 — some researchers propose it as an ancient flute. Its human origin remains debated. One of the oldest known potential musical instruments.

Theories on Music's Origin

Language Extension Theory

Music evolved as an extension of human language and communication — a way of conveying meaning beyond words.

Darwin's Mating Call Theory

Charles Darwin proposed music developed similarly to animal mating calls — functioning as a form of attraction or sexual expression.

Social Cohesion Theory

Music served specific social or biological functions, helping groups bond, coordinate, and maintain cultural identity.

Development of Western Music

The development of Western music is closely linked to the emergence of the staff notation system before the 16th century, enabling composers to record and communicate musical ideas with precision.

From the mid-1500s to the 19th century, musical conventions gradually evolved, leading to more structured forms such as symphonies, concertos, and operas. The term "classical music" came into use in the early 19th century.

Music Through History — Timeline

Ancient Greece
Music was considered essential to education. Believed to cultivate both the mind and soul.
Medieval Europe
Music became central to religious life through Gregorian chant. Also flourished in secular traditions through traveling performers.
Renaissance & Baroque
Increased musical complexity. Rise of composers: Bach, Handel, Vivaldi.
Classical Period
Emphasis on balance and clarity. Key figures: Haydn, Mozart, Beethoven.
Romantic Era
Composers emphasized emotional expression: Chopin, Liszt, Tchaikovsky.
20th–21st Century
More diverse and experimental. Emergence of new genres and technologies. Music as cultural identity.

Music as Performing Art

Core Elements

  • Pitch — melody and harmony
  • Rhythm — tempo, meter, articulation
  • Dynamics — loudness and softness
  • Timbre — unique sound quality of instrument
  • Texture — layering of musical elements

Roles of Music in Society

  • Rituals, celebrations, social events
  • Storytelling and historical preservation
  • Community identity
  • Supporting other arts (dance, theatre)
  • Emotional evocation
Key Insight: Works like Beethoven's Grosse Fuge and even jazz music were once questioned as legitimate forms of music — showing how innovation constantly challenges established conventions.

🎼 Types of Music

No single musical style is inherently superior — each period introduces distinct innovations. Musical genres are strongly shaped by their historical contexts.

1. Western Art Music

Primarily created for an audience of upper-class Europeans. Many genres were produced for upper-class patrons in the 18th and 19th centuries. Characterized by: written notation, complex structures, theoretical foundations.

🎻 Symphony▼

A large-scale composition for orchestra, typically consisting of multiple movements that vary in tempo, mood, and character.

Example: Beethoven: Symphony No. 5, 3rd movement | Paavo Järvi and Deutsche Kammerphilharmonie Bremen

🎹 Sonata▼

A composition usually for a solo instrument or small ensemble. Typically 2–4 movements, each in a related key but with unique musical character.

Derives from the Italian sonare = "to sound" (played on instruments, as opposed to cantata = sung by voices).

Example: Beethoven: Sonata No.21 in C Major, "Waldstein" (Pletnev)

🎵 Concerto▼

A piece featuring a solo musician (or group of soloists) performing WITH an orchestra. The orchestra accompanies and sometimes contrasts the soloist.

Examples: Tchaikovsky: Piano Concerto No. 1, Mov. 3

🎭 Opera▼

A genre combining music, singing, staging (and sometimes dance) to tell a story. Originated in Italy towards the end of the 16th century.

Example: Mozart's The Marriage of Figaro

🩰 Ballet (Music)▼

Specialized orchestral composition created specifically to accompany ballet performances. Provides rhythmic foundation, emotional context, and narrative structures for dancers.

Example: Tchaikovsky – Swan Lake Suite

⭐ Symphony vs Sonata vs Concerto — KEY COMPARISON
FormPerformersScaleKey Feature
SymphonyFull orchestraLargestAll instruments work together, no soloist
ConcertoSoloist + OrchestraMedium-LargeSoloist highlighted; orchestra accompanies
SonataSolo or small groupSmallest/IntimatePersonal, fewer performers

All three often follow a similar structure called sonata form.

2. Folk Music

Composed by local community members. Songs may change over time as community members add/alter lyrics, melodies, or embellishments. Often anonymous — no single composer attached. Passed down orally through generations.

🎵 Ballads▼
Short narrative folk songs. Style crystallized in Europe in the late Middle Ages. Recounts stories about love, history, and mythology. Persists in communities where folk singing remains a tradition. Example: Barbara Allen
⚒️ Work Songs▼
Folk music integrated with manual labor. Oral chronicles of laboring life. Examples: field songs of Black slaves, Cornish sailors' sea shanties, prison/chain gang songs. Help coordinate workers, provide entertainment, express community values.
📜 Epic Songs▼
Long narrative poems, exalted in style, heroic in theme. Focus on legendary adventures of a hero against a historical backdrop. Sung and accompanied by indigenous instruments. Example: The Darangen Epic of the Maranao People
🥁 Drumming▼
Purely instrumental — can take the place of speech to communicate jokes, warnings, and proverbs. Example: West African drumming. Used to put people into spiritual trances. Gives "pulse" or backbone to music. Communal extended sequences with singing and dancing.
🕺 Folk Dance Music▼
Folk dances typically accompanied by traditional or traditionally-inspired music. Rhythms and melodies intricately connected to dancers' movements. Music adds depth and emotion, elevating experience for performers and audience. Example: Philippine Folk Dance: Cariñosa

3. Popular Music

Different from folk music in 3 ways: (1) sold as a commodity for commercial purposes; (2) undergoes more rapid changes in style as fashions come and go; (3) maintains an explicit association with its composers and first performers.

🤠 Country

American popular music originating in rural South and West in early 20th century. Term adopted in 1949 to replace "hillbilly music." Roots in English/Scots/Irish settler ballads and folk songs. Songs about hardship, tragedy, failed relationships. Took off with radio in the 1920s.

Example: Shania Twain

🎭 Musicals

Combines music, dance, and theater. Stories told through spoken dialogue, song, and choreography. Emerged in early 20th century. Known for exploring wide themes — love to political commentary.

Example: Les MisérablesLea Salonga

🎷 Jazz

Known for improvisational nature, rhythmic complexity, unique blend of influences. Originated in early 20th century in New Orleans, Louisiana. Evolved from African American traditions (blues, ragtime) + European elements. Defining feature: improvisation.

Frank SinatraLouis Armstrong

🎤 Rhythm and Blues (R&B)

Dynamic genre emerged in early–mid 1940s. Primarily developed by Black Americans. Fusion of jump blues, traditional blues, jazz, swing, gospel, boogie. Rise coincided with the Great Migration. Evolved into doo-wop, soul, funk, disco, hip-hop.

Ne-YoBeyoncéAretha Franklin

🎸 Rock

Edgy, fast-paced music performed with loud instruments — drums, electric guitars, bass guitars. Traces roots to late 19th–early 20th century styles: blues, gospel, rockabilly, boogie-woogie. Both electric and acoustic guitars central to rock since the beginning.

QueenGuns N' Roses

🎤 Rap

Rhythmic and/or rhyming speech chanted ("rapped") to musical accompaniment. Backing music can include digital sampling — also called hip-hop (broader cultural movement including rap, deejaying, graffiti, breakdancing). Started in the Bronx, NYC in the 1970s.

Grandmaster Flash

🇵🇭 History of Philippine Music

The history of Philippine music is shaped by indigenous roots, three centuries of Spanish influence, American jazz and blues, and contemporary global trends — reflecting the nation's evolving identity and its ties to the spiritual and social.
1. PRE-COLONIAL & INDIGENOUS TRADITIONS
  • Deeply rooted in daily life and traditions of various ethnolinguistic groups
  • Served functional purposes in rituals, agriculture, celebrations, and life-cycle events (birth, courtship, death)
  • Music was largely vocal and improvisational — formal music theory not yet developed
  • Oral traditions central: Darangen of the Maranao, Hudhud of the Ifugao

Soft Instruments (indoor/intimate):

Kudyapi (2-string lute) Korlong (bamboo zither) Tolali (nose flute) Subing (jaw harp)

Loud Instruments (ceremonies/public):

Agong (gong) Drums Bamboo instruments
Regional Differences: Northern Luzon/Cordillera → flat gongs called gangsa. Southern Philippines/Mindanao → kulintang ensemble (gongs, drums, string instruments), reflecting Southeast Asian influences.
Early Trade Influence: Music was also influenced by early trade with Chinese, Arab, and Southeast Asian groups even before Spanish colonization — including spread of Islam in Mindanao.
2. SPANISH COLONIAL INFLUENCE (1521–1898)

Western musical traditions introduced through colonization and Christianization. Spanish missionaries used music as a tool for evangelization.

What was introduced:

  • Gregorian chant
  • Polyphonic music
  • Western notation
  • Various European instruments

Key Musical Forms Emerged:

  • Harana — traditional serenade, courtship music, man expresses love through song (guitar)
  • Kundiman — slow, emotional; love, longing, later patriotism
  • Rondalla — string ensemble of plucked instruments
  • Pasyon — fusion of Catholic teachings and indigenous musical styles
  • Zarzuela — Spanish-derived; alternating sung and spoken scenes; 1–5 acts; first recorded in PH in 1878
  • Kumintang — influenced development of kundiman
  • Cariñosa — localized European dance
  • Pandanggo sa Ilaw — localized European dance
Music became medium for religious devotion AND expressing social concerns, cultural values, and emerging nationalism. Hybrid forms of indigenous + Western music laid foundation for modern Filipino music.
3. AMERICAN PERIOD (1898–1946)
  • Music became part of the formal school curriculum
  • Popular genres introduced: jazz, blues, rock and roll
  • New entertainment forms: radio, musical films, bodabil (Filipino vaudeville) — singing, dancing, comedy, theatrical acts
  • Philippine Constabulary Band under Walter Loving — gained international recognition at the 1904 St. Louis World's Fair
  • Formal music education: conservatories established
  • Notable nationalist composers: Nicanor Abelardo and Francisco Santiago — combined Western musical structures with Filipino themes
  • Anglo-American styles in dance halls/cabarets: ragtime, foxtrot, early jazz
4. JAPANESE PERIOD (1941–1945)
  • American influences strictly banned — Japan suppressing Western culture
  • Music became a tool for propaganda — Japanese songs broadcast on radio, taught in schools
  • Many Filipinos did not fully embrace Japanese musical forms
  • Destruction of cities and widespread suffering limited musical development
  • Some orchestras, symphonies, military marches continued — often state-related
  • Characterized by restriction and control, not innovation
  • Example: "Song of Philippine Independence" — Japanese song about the Philippines (1943)
5. LIBERATION / POST-WAR (After 1946)
  • Period of renewal and growth, rising nationalism after independence in 1946
  • American influence re-emerged: genres like swing, revival of bodabil
  • 1950s–60s: Latin and Western popular styles; samba → cumbachero bands
  • Rock and roll and country gained popularity (influences: Elvis Presley, The Beatles)
  • Rapid development of formal music education — more music schools
  • Republic Act No. 4723 — promoted music education and cultural development
6. THE NEW SOCIETY / MARTIAL LAW (1972–)
  • Martial Law declared September 23, 1972 by Ferdinand E. Marcos Sr. — strict government control on media
  • Imelda Marcos sponsored artists, established the Cultural Center of the Philippines (CCP)
  • Music became powerful tool for resistance and protest
  • Bayan Ko — written by José Corazón de Jesús; popularized during funeral of Benigno Aquino Jr.; symbol of patriotism
  • Freddie Aguilar — "Anak" became an international hit, symbolized Filipino struggles
  • Apo Hiking Society — reflected social realities through music
Music served a dual role: supported by the state as cultural project AND used by the people as protest and voice of resistance.
7. CONTEMPORARY PHILIPPINE MUSIC
  • Dynamic blend of Western influences and local identity
  • 1970s: Manila Sound — Hotdog and VST & Co.; light pop-rock with Taglish lyrics
  • Development of Original Pilipino Music (OPM) and Pinoy Rock
  • Ryan Cayabyab — key role in promoting Filipino music
  • Juan de la Cruz Band — pioneered Pinoy rock movement
  • Festivals: Metro Manila Popular Music Festival (Metro Pop), Himig Awards
  • 1980s–2000s OPM: Regine Velasquez, Gary Valenciano, Eraserheads, Rivermaya
  • Social/political music: Tatsulok by Bamboo (social inequality), Paraiso by Smokey Mountain (environment)
  • Recent rise: P-Pop, indie music, hip-hop communities

🎺 The Orchestral Instruments

⭐ The Four Families — Remember This!
  • 🎻 Strings → add Harmony
  • 🎵 Woodwinds → add Color
  • 🎺 Brass → add Power
  • 🥁 Percussion → add Rhythm
These ensembles work together to produce a dynamic soundscape that tells different tales and evokes different feelings.

1. Woodwind Instruments

Woodwinds are tubes pierced with holes. They produce sound through the vibration of the air column inside the tube. Some woodwinds have reeds — a thin piece of cane that vibrates when blown across.

🪗
Piccolo Highest pitched; mimics sound of birds. NO reed.
🎵
Flute NO reed. Clear, bright tone.
🎶
Clarinet SINGLE reed. Expressive tone. Versatile range.
🎷
Oboe DOUBLE reed. Sad, penetrating tone.
🎸
Bassoon DOUBLE reed. Lowest pitched woodwind.

2. String Instruments

Comprises the most significant section of the orchestra. Crafted by carving, shaping, and gluing wood pieces together — no nails or screws used. Each instrument has four strings. The larger the instrument → lower the sound; smaller → higher the sound. Sound made by plucking or bowing.

🎻
Violin Smallest member of the string family. Highest pitch.
🎻
Viola Slightly larger than violin. Warmer and lower tone.
🎸
Cello Bass family member. Musician must sit to play. Rich, deep tone.
🎼
Double Bass Largest string instrument. Lowest pitch in string family.

3. Brass Instruments

Metallic tubing loops in different lengths, with a mouthpiece at one end and a bell shape at the other. The longer the tubing → lower the sound. Brass players' lips act as reeds. Sound produced by buzzing with the lips while blowing into the mouthpiece. Most have valves pressed and released to change tones.

🎺
Trumpet Bright, powerful sound. Valves for tone changes.
🎵
French Horn Warm, noble sound. Valves. Widely coiled tubing.
🎸
Trombone Uses a slide instead of valves. Deep, powerful tone.
🎶
Tuba Largest and lowest-pitched brass instrument.

4. Percussion Instruments

Made of naturally resonant materials like skin, wood, or metal. Sound produced when the instrument is struck. Role: provide rhythm and character to the orchestra. Range from simple wooden blocks to tuned instruments.

🥁
Bass DrumLarge, deep resonant boom
🎵
Snare DrumSharp, crisp sound with snare wires
🔔
Timpani/KettledrumTuned drums, pedal adjusts pitch
🎶
XylophoneTuned wooden bars, mallet struck
🎵
CymbalsMetallic clash; dramatic accents
🔔
GlockenspielTuned metal bars, bright bell sound
🥁
Bongo DrumsHand percussion, two drums of different sizes
🎹
PianoPercussion by hammers striking strings

💃 Dance as Art

What is Dance?

Dance is an art form that involves the rhythmic movement of the body, often performed to music within a given space, for expression, communication, or enjoyment. More than simple movement, dance is characterized by its expressiveness — it conveys ideas, emotions, and experiences rather than serving purely functional actions.

It is both a physical and artistic activity where the body becomes a medium for storytelling and creative expression. While dance may begin as a natural human impulse to move, it becomes an art when shaped through skill, technique, and intention.

Dance transcends language and cultural boundaries, making it a universal mode of communication. Through choreography, gestures, and emotion, it has the power to tell stories, evoke feelings, and connect performers with audiences on a deeper level. Dance is also a multisensory experience — combining movement with music, costumes, and visual elements.

Why Do People Dance?

🙏 Spiritual / Religious

Dance has been closely connected to religion and spirituality — serving as means of worship, ritual, and communication with higher powers. Early societies used dance to honor natural elements (sun and rain), believing movements could invoke blessings.

👥 Social / Community

Dance brings people together, fostering companionship, cooperation, and shared experiences. Often present in celebrations, courtship, and communal gatherings. Helps strengthen relationships and maintain traditions within societies.

🏃 Personal Benefits

Contributes to physical, mental, and emotional well-being. Form of exercise improving coordination, balance, and health. Offers creative outlet for self-expression. Brings enjoyment, fulfillment, stress relief, and happiness.

Dance in Philippine Context

Dance has been an essential part of Filipino culture even before foreign colonization. It reflects the country's rich history, diverse traditions, and the everyday lives of its people. Across the Philippine archipelago, dances developed as expressions of beliefs, social practices, and cultural identity.

🩰 Popular Types of Dance

1. 🩰 Ballet▼
  • Classical dance form known for precise, graceful, and controlled movements
  • Emphasizes strength, flexibility, discipline, and storytelling through choreography and music
  • Originated during the Italian Renaissance (15th century); later developed in France and Russia
  • Evolved into a concert dance form performed on stage worldwide
  • Known for en pointe — female dancers on tips of toes
  • Includes: classical, romantic, neoclassical, contemporary ballet styles
  • Technique requires: strict body alignment, turned-out legs, specific arm positions
Cecchetti method Bournonville method Vaganova method French School Royal Academy Balanchine method
2. 👫 Ballroom▼
  • Partner dance genre — elegant, coordinated movements between two dancers
  • Emphasizes grace, rhythm, and synchronization between partners
  • Originated in European royal courts (France), late 16th century
  • Also known as dancesport
  • Technique: proper posture, strong frame, clear communication between partners
Standard/Smooth: Waltz, Tango, Foxtrot
Latin/Rhythm: Cha-cha, Samba, Pasodoble, Bolero
3. 🌊 Contemporary▼
  • Modern, expressive dance form emphasizing creativity and individual interpretation
  • Combines elements from classical ballet, modern dance, and jazz
  • Known for freedom of movement — departs from strict traditional techniques
  • Developed in the mid-20th century as reaction against rigidity of classical ballet
  • Key choreographers: Martha Graham and Merce Cunningham
  • Technique: fluidity, improvisation, natural use of gravity
  • Movements: contract and release, fall and recovery, extensive floor work
  • Dancers typically perform barefoot
4. 🔥 Hip Hop Dance▼
  • Dynamic, energetic street dance style associated with hip-hop music and culture
  • Originated in the 1970s in the Bronx, New York City and parts of California
  • Developed from African American and Latino cultural movements, influenced by funk music and breakbeats
  • Emphasizes individuality, creativity, and self-expression
  • Technique: body isolation, rhythmic precision, strong musicality
  • Features dance battles
Breaking (breakdance) Locking Popping Memphis Jookin' Turfing Krumping
5. 🎶 Jazz Dance▼
  • Social and performance dance style rooted in African and African American traditions
  • Combines African movement styles with European dance influences
  • Closely connected to jazz music development
  • Originated in 17th century through African traditions brought via the Atlantic slave trade
  • Developed further in early 20th century in African American communities
  • Characterized by improvisation, rhythmic energy, expressive body movements
  • Known for dramatic, energetic, theatrical performance quality
CharlestonCakewalk SwingLindy HopShimmy
6. 👞 Tap Dance▼
  • Percussive dance form — creates rhythm and sound through footwork
  • Dancers use metal plates attached to heels and toes of tap shoes to produce distinct sounds
  • Originated in the United States during the 19th century
  • Developed through fusion of African rhythmic traditions and Irish step dance
  • Technique: intricate footwork, syncopated rhythms, improvisation
  • Aims to produce clear, crisp, and hollow sounds
  • Often performed in musical theatre
Rhythm tap Broadway tap Classical tap Postmodern tap
7. 🌾 Folk Dance▼
  • Traditional form reflecting cultural heritage of a specific community or region
  • Expresses shared identity through movement, music, and storytelling
  • Performed during social events, rituals, festivals, and historical celebrations
  • Originates from long-established community practices passed down through generations
  • Movements incorporate symbolism, storytelling, and local customs
  • Encourages participation, cultural pride, and communal bonding
Bharatanatyam (India) Samba (Brazil) Hula (Hawaii)
8. ☘️ Irish Dance▼
  • Traditional dance known for rapid and precise leg and foot movements
  • Characterized by stiff, straight upper body and highly intricate footwork
  • Originated in Ireland with roots dating back to pre-Christian times
  • Developed through centuries of cultural, social, and religious influences
  • Gained international popularity through Riverdance
  • Includes complex jumps, turns, synchronized foot patterns
Soft shoe — light, flexible movements
Hard shoe — percussive, tap-like using jig shoes
9. 🎨 Modern Dance▼
  • Expressive dance form emerged in the early 20th century
  • Developed as reaction against rigid structure of classical ballet
  • Focuses on freedom of movement, creativity, emotional expression
  • Originated in Western countries, particularly USA and Germany
  • Pioneered by Isadora Duncan and Martha Graham
  • Technique emphasizes natural, grounded movement
  • Common elements: contraction and release, spirals, floor work, off-center movements
  • Relies heavily on interpretation and improvisation
10. 🕺 Swing Dance▼
  • Group of partner dances developed alongside swing jazz music
  • Known for energetic, upbeat, and playful movements
  • Originated in the United States during the 1920s and 1930s
  • Developed during the swing era in ballrooms and clubs
  • Evolved from jazz and tap dance influences
  • Technique: strong partner connection, rhythmic footwork, improvisation
Lindy Hop Charleston Jitterbug Balboa

🇵🇭 History of Dance in the Philippines

Dance has been an essential part of Filipino culture even before foreign colonization. It reflects the country's rich history, diverse traditions, and everyday lives of its people.
1. PRE-COLONIAL & INDIGENOUS DANCES
  • Early Filipino tribes across the islands practiced their own unique dances
  • Maguindanao and Maranao — created the Sagayan Dance to honor Prince Bantugan; celebrates his bravery and adventures
  • Indigenous groups like the Igorot expressed their connection to nature through movement
  • Dances imitated natural elements: birds, wind, the earth
  • Performed for rituals, worship, thanksgiving, and agricultural celebrations
  • Many dances passed down orally through generations
2. ISLAMIC INFLUENCE IN MINDANAO
  • Islam introduced in the 12th century through traders and seafarers
  • Muslim communities ("Moros" — Muslim Filipinos in Mindanao and Sulu) integrated religion into dance
  • Dances are graceful, symbolic, often tell stories of nature and life
  • Costumes: elaborate with jewels for women; weapons (swords and shields) for men
  • Accompanied by kulintang (gong ensemble) music
  • Traditions remained strong due to resistance to Spanish colonization
Singkil — A Maranao court dance from Lanao del Sur. Famous for precise footwork between clacking bamboo poles, ornate royal costuming, and music led by the kulintang gong ensemble.
3. SPANISH COLONIAL PERIOD (1521–1898)
  • Spanish colonization introduced Christianity and Western cultural influences
  • European dances adopted: waltz, fandango, polka
  • Dances were localized and blended with Filipino style
  • Fusion led to development of the Maria Clara dances — named after a character in Noli Me Tangere
  • Dances reflected elegance, social class, and Hispanic influence
Cariñosa — Beloved Philippine courtship dance shaped by Spanish influence. Danced with a fan and handkerchief to lilting 3/4 music. Teaches playful pursuit, modesty, and social grace.
4. RURAL & FESTIVE DANCES (BARRIO TRADITIONS)
  • Dance became part of community celebrations, especially in rural areas
  • Farmers performed dances after work during fiestas and gatherings
  • Common props: bamboo poles, candles, hats, benches
  • Pandanggo sa Ilaw ("dance of lights") — dancers balance oil lamps to imitate fireflies
  • Sayaw sa Salakot — dance using embellished wide-brimmed rattan/reed hats traditionally worn by farmers
  • These dances highlight joy, creativity, and communal bonding
5. NATIONAL DANCE: TINIKLING
  • Considered one of the oldest and most iconic Philippine folk dances
  • Originated from Leyte Province
  • Inspired by the movements of the "tinikling" bird avoiding bamboo traps set by farmers
  • Dancers skillfully step in and out of moving bamboo poles
  • Symbolizes agility, coordination, and harmony with nature
  • Some stories link its origin to colonial punishment — later transformed into art
⭐ Remember: Tinikling = National Folk Dance of the Philippines
Mimic movement of tikling birds hopping over trees, grass stems, or bamboo traps set by farmers.

🎭 Elements of Dance

1. Body

The basic component of dance. How the body initiates movement. Inner self-perceptions, emotions, imaginations, thoughts, and intentions are all classified as "body."

The human body is what others see when they look at dance. A dancer can use the whole body or emphasize individual body parts. Dancers use their bodies to:

  • Take internal ideas/emotions
  • Express them in an outward manner

2. Theme

An essential element — the message conveyed by the dance. The central idea, story, or emotion the dance communicates to the audience.

3. Design

The plan or organization of movement in time and space. How movements are structured and arranged for artistic effect.

4. Elements of Movement

The medium of dance — how movement is shaped and executed

a. Space

Area or environment — where a dancer moves. Includes direction, levels, pathways, and formations on stage. Dancers may stay in one place or travel from place to place.

b. Time

Determines the rhythm, tempo, and duration of movements. Includes clock time, sensed time, and event-sequence.

c. Duration

The length of time for movements or a performance.

d. Force

The energy applied to movement.

e. Dynamics

The force of movement — strong, soft, sharp, flowing.

Additional Elements

5. Techniques

Refers to the skills of executing movement. The technical proficiency and training behind the physical execution.

6. Music

Motivates the movement of dancers. Melodic and harmonious accompaniment background that guides rhythm, emotion, and energy.

7. Costume and Props

Enhances the effect of the dance. Reflective of the customs, beliefs, and environment of the people. Props: fans, candles, hats, bamboo poles, etc.

8. Choreography

The forms and arrangement of movements. The art of designing sequences of dance movements.

9. Scenery

The setting or background and place of action for the dance performance.
